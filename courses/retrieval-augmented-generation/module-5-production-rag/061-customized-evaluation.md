## What Is a Customized Dataset?

Standard benchmark datasets — curated question-answer pairs drawn from public corpora — are a useful starting point for evaluating a RAG system, but they have a fundamental limitation: they are not your data. The questions in a benchmark were written by researchers, not your users. The documents in a benchmark came from Wikipedia or news archives, not your knowledge base. As a result, a strong score on a benchmark can coexist with poor real-world performance, and a weak benchmark score may not reveal the specific failure modes your users are actually hitting.

A **customized evaluation dataset** solves this mismatch by drawing directly from the live traffic your system processes. At its simplest, it is a collection of prompts your system has previously handled, together with any metadata you choose to capture about how those prompts flowed through the pipeline. Because the data comes from real users asking real questions against your real knowledge base, evaluations run on it are far more predictive of production quality than synthetic benchmarks.

The value of a custom dataset is twofold. First, it gives you a retrospective window into how the system performed in the past — you can audit responses that generated complaints, identify edge cases, and measure quality on slices of traffic that matter most to your business. Second, it gives you a prospective experiment platform: when you redesign a component (say, you swap your retriever or modify your re-ranker prompt), you can run the new configuration against the same historical prompts and directly compare performance before any change reaches users.

Building this kind of dataset is not a one-time exercise. It is an ongoing practice that grows richer as your system accumulates traffic, and it is the foundation on which robust production evaluation rests. See [[050-evaluating-your-llms-performance]] for the broader context in which customized datasets sit.

## Deciding What Data to Store

The first design question when building a custom dataset is deceptively simple: *what should I log?* The answer is equally simple in principle — log whatever you intend to evaluate — but the practical implication is that you must think about your evaluation goals before you write a single line of logging code.

At a minimum, nearly every RAG system should capture:

- **The input prompt** submitted by the user. This is the seed of everything that follows.
- **The final response** generated by the system. Together with the prompt, it gives you a coarse picture of end-to-end quality.

These two fields alone are enough to support basic human review, LLM-as-judge scoring, and A/B comparisons between system versions. If you are just starting out, capturing prompt and response is far better than capturing nothing.

Beyond that baseline, the richness of what you store is limited only by engineering effort and storage cost. Useful additional fields include:

- **Retrieved document chunks** — the raw passages your vector database returned, before any re-ranking.
- **Re-ranked document chunks** — the ordered list after your re-ranker has run, so you can compare the two orderings and measure how much re-ranking changed retrieval quality.
- **Query rewrites** — the expanded or reformulated queries produced by your query-rewriting component, if you use one.
- **Router decisions** — in agentic workflows with multiple specialized sub-systems, which branch the router chose for each prompt.
- **Latency and token counts** — operational metrics that matter for cost and user experience.
- **User or session identifiers** — so you can segment performance by customer, cohort, or session type.
- **Feedback signals** — thumbs-up/down ratings, follow-up questions, or escalations that serve as implicit quality signals.

In practice, the logging tables for a production RAG system frequently reach dozens of columns. This breadth is intentional: it gives you the flexibility to slice the data in many directions later, rather than discovering that you needed a field you never logged. The cost of over-logging is storage; the cost of under-logging is a blind spot you may not notice until a production incident forces it to your attention.

## Component-Level Logging

End-to-end logging — prompt in, response out — tells you whether the system is working but not *why* it is or is not working. To diagnose root causes, you need **component-level logging**: capturing the input and output of each major component in your pipeline individually.

Consider the structure of a typical RAG pipeline. A user prompt enters the system and may be rewritten by a query-rewriter. The rewritten query is sent to a retriever, which returns a ranked list of document chunks from the vector database. Those chunks are passed to a re-ranker that produces a new ordering. The top-k chunks are assembled into a context window and sent with the original (or rewritten) prompt to the generation LLM, which produces the final response. In an agentic system, a router may have dispatched the prompt to a specialized sub-agent before any of this occurred.

Each transition between components is a potential failure point. Component-level logging makes each one inspectable:

- If the **retriever** returns irrelevant chunks, the generation LLM has no basis for a good answer, and logging the retrieved chunks will surface this.
- If the **re-ranker** demotes the most relevant chunk to position six, and you only pass the top three to generation, the re-ranker is the culprit — but only component-level logs of before-and-after rankings will reveal it.
- If the **query rewriter** introduces a term that steers retrieval toward the wrong domain, the rewritten queries in your logs will show this.
- If a **router** in an agentic workflow misclassifies a prompt and sends it to the wrong sub-system, router decision logs will make the mismatch visible.

The diagnostic power of this granularity is substantial. Suppose you are building a customer service chatbot and you notice that questions about product delays consistently receive low-quality responses. You filter your logs to that topic. You see that the retriever returns few relevant documents for those queries. You check your knowledge base and realize it contains almost no content about shipping timelines and delay policies. The fix is clear: enrich the knowledge base with the missing content. Without component-level logging, you would know only that the system underperforms on delay questions — not where in the pipeline the breakdown occurs.

This connects directly to the evaluation framework covered in [[058-implementing-rag-evaluation-strategies]], where per-component metrics (retrieval precision, re-ranker NDCG, answer faithfulness) are used alongside end-to-end measures.

## Observability in Practice: A Case Study

The abstract case for logging becomes concrete when you encounter a production incident. Consider a real-world example from a specialized RAG system that could generate text, images, and code-backed charts and diagrams using MermaidJS.

Users began reporting that some of the diagrams produced by the system were low quality. The team opened their logs and worked backwards through the pipeline. They filtered to prompts that had generated diagram-related complaints and inspected the router decision column. A pattern emerged immediately: when users asked the system to "draw a diagram," the router language model was mis-classifying these requests as image-generation tasks and forwarding them to the text-to-image model.

The text-to-image model is proficient at generating photorealistic images of objects and people, but it has no concept of structured diagram syntax. When asked to "draw a diagram of the authentication flow," it produced a literal picture rather than a MermaidJS flowchart. The result looked nothing like the structured chart the user wanted.

The fix was straightforward: update the system prompt for the router LLM so that requests containing diagram-related language were routed to the code-generation path (MermaidJS) instead of the image-generation path. Once the fix was deployed, the team could verify its effectiveness by re-running the problematic historical prompts through the updated system and comparing router decisions in the logs.

This example illustrates several principles simultaneously:

1. **Complaints are lagging indicators.** Users noticed the problem before the team did. Robust logging means that once a complaint arrives, the investigation is fast — you already have all the data.
2. **The failure was in a component, not at the end.** End-to-end logs would have shown "user asked for diagram, system produced bad image." Only component-level logs (specifically, the router decision column) revealed *why*.
3. **Logs enable verification, not just diagnosis.** After the fix, the same historical prompts served as a regression test.

The broader lesson is that the return on investment for a well-designed observability system is highest at exactly the moment you need it most: when something goes wrong in production and you need to move fast.

## Visualizing and Clustering Logged Data

Once you have accumulated a substantial log of production traffic, individual prompt inspection gives way to aggregate analysis. Visualizing your data is often the most efficient way to find systemic patterns that no single prompt would reveal.

One particularly powerful technique is **prompt clustering**. Embed all logged input prompts using a text embedding model and then apply a clustering algorithm (k-means, HDBSCAN, or similar) to group semantically similar prompts together. Each cluster typically represents a coherent topic or intent category: "questions about refunds," "questions about product availability," "troubleshooting requests," "onboarding questions," and so on.

Clustering serves two evaluation purposes:

**Discovering topic coverage.** If a cluster is large and growing, your users care a lot about that topic. If your knowledge base has thin coverage of that cluster, you have found a gap worth addressing. You can also detect emerging topics — new clusters that appear after a product launch or a news event — and proactively add relevant content before performance degrades.

**Targeted evaluation runs.** Once you have labeled clusters, you can run your evaluation pipeline on a specific cluster in isolation. This tells you whether the system is underperforming for a particular topic without the signal being diluted by better performance on other topics. If your overall quality score is 0.82 but quality on delay-related questions is 0.54, a cluster-filtered eval surfaces that gap; an aggregate eval may hide it.

Visualization can go beyond prompt clustering. You can plot quality scores over time to detect regressions after deployments. You can chart retrieval hit rates by document collection to see which parts of your knowledge base are being used. You can map latency distributions to find tail-latency outliers. Together, these views give you a continuous dashboard of system health rather than a snapshot taken at deployment time.

## Integrating Custom Datasets Into Your Evaluation Loop

A custom dataset is only as valuable as the evaluation processes that use it. The goal is to integrate your logged production data into a repeatable, automated evaluation loop — one that runs on a schedule, runs before deployments, and produces actionable signals rather than raw scores.

A practical integration looks like this:

1. **Continuous logging.** Every prompt processed in production is written to a structured log store (a database, a data warehouse, or a dedicated observability platform). Component-level fields are captured alongside prompt and response.

2. **Sampling for evaluation.** Because running an LLM-as-judge evaluation on every logged prompt is expensive, you sample strategically: a random sample for overall health monitoring, plus targeted samples from underperforming clusters or from prompts that received low user ratings.

3. **Evaluation pipeline execution.** The sampled prompts are run through your evaluation pipeline — the same pipeline described in [[058-implementing-rag-evaluation-strategies]] — which applies your chosen metrics (faithfulness, context precision, answer relevance) and returns per-prompt scores.

4. **Dashboarding and alerting.** Scores are aggregated and visualized. Thresholds trigger alerts when quality drops below acceptable levels for any cluster or component.

5. **Experiment comparison.** When you are considering a change to the system (a new retriever, a different re-ranker, a modified prompt), you run the candidate configuration against the same historical prompts and compare score distributions before promoting the change.

This closed loop — log, sample, evaluate, visualize, act — is the operational core of a mature RAG quality program. It transforms evaluation from a one-time pre-launch check into an ongoing engineering discipline that keeps the system aligned with real user needs as those needs evolve.

Custom datasets are the bridge between the controlled conditions of development-time evaluation and the messy reality of production traffic. Incorporating them into how you measure and improve your system is not merely a best practice — it is the only way to be confident that your system is actually serving the users it was built for.

## Post-test

1. You are building a RAG-powered customer support tool. After deployment, users complain that responses to billing questions are poor. Describe the step-by-step investigation you would conduct using your component-level logs to identify the root cause.

2. A colleague proposes logging only the final prompt and final response to save storage costs. What is the strongest argument against this approach, and what additional fields would you prioritize adding to the log schema?

3. You have clustered 50,000 logged prompts into 12 topic groups. Three clusters have average quality scores above 0.85, but one cluster (shipping delays) has an average score of 0.48. What actions would you take, and in what order?

> [!example]- Answer Guide
> 
> #### Q1 — Investigating Billing Query Failures
> 
> Start by filtering logs to prompts classified under the billing topic (either via manual tagging or cluster membership). For each such prompt, inspect the retrieved document chunks column. If retrieved chunks are irrelevant to billing, the retriever is the bottleneck — check whether the knowledge base contains adequate billing documentation and whether the embedding model handles billing terminology well. If retrieved chunks are relevant but re-ranked chunks demote the most useful ones, the re-ranker is mis-weighting billing content. If both retrieval and re-ranking look correct but the final response is still poor, inspect the generation LLM's output against the context provided — the problem may lie in the prompt template or in how context is assembled. Follow each signal until you reach the component where the breakdown occurs.
> 
> #### Q2 — Against Logging Only Endpoints
> 
> The primary weakness of logging only endpoints is that it tells you *what* went wrong but not *why*. When a response is poor, there is no way to distinguish between a retrieval failure (the knowledge base returned irrelevant chunks), a re-ranking failure (the correct chunk was retrieved but demoted), a query-rewriting failure (the rewrite steered retrieval off-target), or a generation failure (the LLM had good context but produced a poor response). Without component-level data, every debugging session starts from scratch. The highest-priority additional fields are: retrieved document chunks (before re-ranking), re-ranked document chunks (after re-ranking), and — in agentic systems — the router's decision for each prompt.
> 
> #### Q3 — Acting on Low-Scoring Cluster
> 
> First, pull the shipping-delays cluster logs and inspect retrieved chunks: if the retriever is returning few or no relevant documents, the most impactful fix is to enrich the knowledge base with shipping and delay policy content. Second, if retrieval looks adequate but re-ranked ordering is poor, audit the re-ranker on a sample of delay-related prompts to see whether it is systematically mis-scoring that content type. Third, if retrieval and re-ranking are both reasonable, examine the generation LLM's responses against the provided context to identify prompt-template issues. Finally, set an automated alert so that the shipping-delays cluster score is monitored continuously — any regression after future deployments will be caught immediately rather than discovered via user complaints.
