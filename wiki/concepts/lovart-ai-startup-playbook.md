# Lovart: An AI Startup Playbook from Chen Mian

A 3.5-hour conversation between Lovart founder Chen Mian and Luo Yonghao that goes far beyond product demo -- it traces a complete founder formation arc and distills a reusable AI-era startup methodology.

## The Founder's Formation

Chen Mian's career path (Tencent, 360, Baidu, Didi, Mobike, MissFresh, ByteDance) looks like job-hopping on paper. His reframe: if your goal function is becoming a founder, every role is evaluated by whether it builds the capabilities you will need -- organizational understanding, competitive awareness, product rhythm, capital logic. The decade in big tech was not a career but a training ground.

AI arrived as "redemption" -- a new time window for someone who had repeatedly approached opportunity in the mobile era only to be pushed back by structural forces.

## Core Product Thesis: High-Value Professional Needs

Chen Mian chose the visual/multimodal direction not because it was the hottest, but because it is more likely to form vertical commercial opportunity while avoiding head-on collision with giants on general assistants and foundation models.

The critical distinction: **professional needs vs. professional people**. Lovart serves professional-grade design needs -- which may come from designers, clients, or non-professional creators -- but the needs themselves must be deep and valuable enough to sustain a business. "Many people try it once" is not the same as "many people cannot do without it."

## Do Not Sit Downstream of Someone Else's Workflow

This is the playbook's sharpest insight. Two rules:

1. **Do not position yourself downstream** of a workflow giants already control
2. **If you are replacing a role, not just a feature**, your product form must match how people actually collaborate with that role

Lovart is built as a canvas-based, collaborative, AI-native design tool -- not a chat box wrapping a model. If the goal is "AI designer," the interaction must mirror how humans work with designers: canvas, visual alignment, artifact continuity, co-editing.

## The Window of Half-Belief

The ideal startup battlefield is not where giants have zero awareness, but where they can see the opportunity but are not yet fully committed. Pure blindspots may not be real opportunities. The exploitable gap is **cognitive difference and conviction difference** -- you believe something is true before they do.

## Survival Is Not Just Product Quality

Chen Mian's hardest lesson: "Good product means funding will come" is a dangerous illusion. Early mistakes included low valuation, too many early investors, insufficient cash reserves, and assuming the next round would always arrive. The company nearly died. Fundraising capability, cash flow management, investor structure, and competitive timing are first-order survival questions alongside product.

## Speed as Competitive Advantage

In the AI era, this is not a "build one product generation every 3-5 years" game. Chen Mian's position: **speed is all you need**. As long as model scaling continues, the application layer keeps being rewritten. You do not think clearly first and then act -- you continuously correct understanding while moving at high velocity. This connects to the rapid iteration patterns described in [[harness-engineering]].

## Creative Labor in the AI Era

AI will compress the scarcity of execution-type, style-replication work. But before AGI, the most valuable human contribution shifts to **creating new taste, new styles, new expressive directions**. The harder unsolved problem: even if new human aesthetics retain value, society may not automatically reward creators fairly. This is both a product problem and an institutional problem.

## Condensed Decision Framework

Before committing to a direction, answer:
- Is this a high-value need, or just surface-level excitement?
- Am I sitting downstream of someone else's workflow?
- Can my current strengths get me in the door?
- What is my moat after scaling?
- How long can I survive if fundraising stalls?
- If giants enter, why can I not be copied to death?
