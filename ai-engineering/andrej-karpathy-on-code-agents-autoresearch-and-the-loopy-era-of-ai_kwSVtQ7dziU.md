## Key Points
- Karpathy describes a major workflow shift since around December 2025: from mostly writing code directly to mostly delegating implementation to coding agents.
- He frames current performance limits as often a "skill issue" in orchestration, instruction quality, and parallelization strategy, rather than pure model incapability.
- "Mastery" is moving from single-agent chat to multi-agent coordination, persistent loops ("claws"), memory systems, and long-running background execution.
- He argues model capabilities are "jagged": rapid progress in verifiable domains (coding, optimization) does not automatically transfer to all human-like abilities (for example humor).
- On model ecosystem structure, he expects continued coexistence: frontier closed models at the edge and open-source models trailing by months but covering broad practical use cases.
- He sees digital knowledge work changing first and fastest; physical-world robotics and automation should progress more slowly because atoms are harder than bits.
- For jobs, he leans cautiously optimistic for software demand in the near term (Jevons-style effect): cheaper software creation can increase total demand for software.
- He presents AutoResearch as an attempt to close the loop on research work (experiment, training, optimization), while highlighting risks like over-optimizing to narrow metrics.
- In education, he predicts a shift toward "agent-readable" artifacts and curricula, where humans encode key insights and agents handle most explanation/personalization.

## Takeaways
- Practical leverage now comes from agent orchestration skill: parallelism, decomposition, memory scaffolding, and review discipline.
- Expect uneven capability growth; product and workflow design should assume jagged strengths, not uniform intelligence.
- Near-term opportunity is largest in digital workflows; physical automation likely follows behind.
- Open + closed model coexistence is likely to remain strategically important for both innovation and ecosystem health.
- Education and documentation are shifting from human-first delivery to agent-mediated delivery, with humans focusing on non-obvious high-value insights.
